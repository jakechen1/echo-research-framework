# From experimentation to engagement

The research paper "From experimentation to engagement" investigates the growing disparity between the implementation of [[from-experimentation-to-engagement-on-the-paradox-of-participatory-ai-and-power-|participatory AI]] in the [[global-north|Global North]] and its absence in the [[global-south|Global South]], specifically within the context of [[humanitarian-crises|humanitarian crises]] and [[forced-displacement|forced displacement]]. 

While initiatives such as community consultations, citizens' assemblies, and co-design processes are increasingly used in wealthier nations to ensure [[ethical-ai|ethical AI]] and safety, these participatory frameworks are rarely deployed in the regions where the deployment of [[algorithmic-tools|algorithmic tools]] is accelerating most rapidly.

## Key Findings and "Participation Washing"

Based on a pilot study conducted in the [[kakuma-refugee-camp|Kakuma Refugee Camp]] in Kenya, the authors identify significant limitations in current participatory approaches. A primary risk highlighted is "**participation washing**"—a process where superficial engagement is used to manufacture consent for AI deployment without addressing the substantive risks of [[algorithmic-harm|algorithmic harm]] to vulnerable populations.

The study posits that the primary driver of these risks is not a lack of technical awareness or digital literacy among displaced persons. Instead, the authors argue that thedanger lies in the fundamental [[power-dynamics|power dynamics]] inherent in the humanitarian sector. These structural inequalities exist between:

*   **Aid Recipients and Service Providers:** The asymmetrical relationship between those receiving aid and those controlling the flow of resources.
*   **Donor Governments and Host Nations:** The geopolitical pressures that influence how technology is deployed in semi-permanent settlements.
*   **AI Companies and Humanitarian Actors:** The imbalance of power and differing incentives between commercial technology developers and non-governmental organizations.

## Conclusion and Recommendations

The paper concludes that simply improving "participation" is insufficient to protect displaced communities. To mitigate the risks of exploitation, the authors argue for the creation of an [[independent-governance-architecture|independent governance architecture]]. Such a system would be capable of holding [[humanitarian-ai|humanitarian AI]] to account, ensuring that the deployment of technology does not bypass the agency and safety of the world's most vulnerable populations.
