# Governing frontier general-purpose AI in the public sector

The governance of [[Frontier AI]] systems represents a critical challenge for [[Institutional Design]] within the [[Public Sector]]. As outlined in this article, the management of general-purpose AI transitioned from being a purely technical matter of model performance to a complex sociotechnical problem involving organizational routines, data arrangements, and public values.

## The Evidence Dilemma
The article identifies a profound "evidence dilemma" driven by the asymmetry between technological progress and regulatory knowledge. While AI capabilities are advancing rapidly and unevenly, scientific understanding regarding potential harms, safety safeguards, and effective interventions remains partial and lagged. This creates an environment of extreme [[Technological Uncertainty]], where policymakers must formulate strategies for the period leading up to 2030 without a clear view of all possible technological trajectories.

## From Static Compliance to Adaptive Management
A central thesis of the paper is that traditional, static compliance models—which rely on fixed rules and periodic audits—are insufficient for the rapidly evolving AI landscape. Instead, the authors propose a framework centered on [[Adaptive Risk Management]] and [[Sociotechnical Transformation]]. This framework integrates several key components:

*   **Capability Monitoring:** Continuous tracking of technical advancements to inform policy.
*   **Risk Tiering:** Categorizing AI applications based on the severity of potential impacts.
*   **Conditional Controls:** Implementing safeguards that act dynamically based on known risks.
*   **Institutional Learning:** Building the capacity for public organizations to incorporate new evidence into existing structures.

## Conclusion
Ultimately, the successful integration and oversight of AI in government functions depend on significant organizational redesign and increased [[Policy Capacity]]. Effective [[AI Governance]] requires mechanisms that are not only robust against technical failure but also resilient enough to remain functional across divergent and unpredictable technological futures.
