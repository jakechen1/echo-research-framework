The concept of utilizing decommissioned or "old" laptops as low-cost servers within a [[colocation]] (colo) environment presents an innovative approach to [[almab-dc-active-learning-multi-armed-bandits-and-distributed-computing-for-seque|distributed computing]] and [[sustainable-computing|sustainable computing]]. Traditionally, hardware deployment in a [[datacenter]] relies on expensive, enterprise-grade [[server-hardware|server hardware]]. However, as laptop lifecycles evolve, a significant amount of capable processing power often becomes [[e-waste]].

One of the primary advantages of this approach is the built-in redundancy provided by laptop batteries, which act as an integrated [[uninterruptible-power-supply|Uninterruptible Power Supply]] (UPS). This can safeguard against brief power fluctuations without the immediate need for external power conditioning equipment. Furthermore, the low power consumption of modern mobile CPUs makes this an attractive option for [[multi-turn-reasoning-llms-for-task-offloading-in-mobile-edge-computing|edge computing]] workloads where energy efficiency is a critical metric for maintaining low operational costs.

Despite the economic benefits, several engineering challenges must be addressed. Unlike standard rack-mount servers, laptops are not natively designed for high-density stacking. Managing thermal dissipation and airflow within a [[server-rack|server rack]] is critical to prevent overheating and hardware degradation.
