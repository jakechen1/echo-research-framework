# "When to Hand Off, When to Work Together"

The research article **"When to Hand Off, When to Work Together"** explores a paradigm shift in [[human-agent-collaboration|Human-Agent Collaboration]]. As [[artificial-intelligence-and-the-structure-of-mathematics|Artificial Intelligence]] agents transition from background tools to active participants in shared digital workspaces, the nature of interaction is moving away from traditional [[sequential-delegation|Sequential Delegation]] toward a model of **concurrent co-creation**.

## The Coordination Problem
In traditional workflows, a human delegates a task and waits for completion. However, as agent execution becomes visible in real-time, users are naturally prompted to intervene while the agent is still working. This visibility creates a fundamental coordination problem. The authors identify a critical "capability gap" in current [[undetectable-conversations-between-ai-agents-via-pseudorandom-noise-resilient-ke|AI Agents]]: the inability to maintain [[context-awareness|Context Awareness]]. Specifically, agents often struggle to distinguish whether a user's simultaneous action in the workspace is a piece of instructional feedback or simply the user performing independent, parallel work.

## The CLEO Design Probe
To address this gap, the researchers developed **CLEO**, a design probe engineered to embody advanced collaborative intelligence. CLEO is designed to interpret concurrent user actions by determining whether they function as feedback intended to steer the agent or as autonomous tasks. This allows for a more fluid, adaptive execution pattern that supports true [[co-creation|Co-Creation]].

## Research Findings and Taxonomy
Through two primary studies (N=20 total), the researchers analyzed the dynamics of this new interaction model:

* **Study 1:** Confirmed that process visibility triggers concurrent intervention but highlighted the failure of agents to differentiate between feedback and parallel tasks.
* **Study 2:** Involved the analysis of 214 turn-level interactions, resulting in a comprehensive taxonomy for the field, including:
    * **Five Action Patterns** and **Ten Codes** describing interaction types.
    * **Six Triggers** and **Four Enabling Factors** that dictate when users shift between delegation and co-creation.

The study found that **concurrent interaction** accounted for **31.8%** of all interaction turns. The authors conclude that mastering concurrent interaction is the key to making [[compiled-ai-deterministic-code-generation-for-llm-based-workflow-automation|Automation]] and delegation more effective in complex, shared environments.
