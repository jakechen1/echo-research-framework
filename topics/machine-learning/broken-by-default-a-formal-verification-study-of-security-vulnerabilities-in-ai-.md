# Broken by Default: A Formal Verification Study of Security Vulnerables in AI-Generated Code

The research paper **"Broken by Default"** presents a rigorous, mathematical evaluation of the security reliability of [[Artificial Intelligence]] coding assistants. As [[Generative AI]] becomes increasingly integrated into the [[Software Development]] lifecycle, the potential for introducing critical [[Cybersecurity]] vulnerabilities into production environments becomes a significant concern.

## Methodology

The study moves beyond traditional [[Static Analysis]], which often relies on pattern-based heuristics that can be bypassed. Instead, the researchers utilized the **COBALT analysis pipeline** to perform [[Formal Verification]] on 3,500 code artifacts generated by seven frontier [[Large Language Models]] (LLMs). 

By employing the [[Z3 SMT solver]], the study generated mathematical satisfiability witnesses to provide concrete proof of vulnerabilities. The dataset was comprised of 500 security-critical prompts covering five distinct Common Weakness Enumeration (CWE) categories.

## Key Findings

The study reveals a "broken by default" state for current models:

* **High Vulnerability Prevalence:** Across all tested models, an average of 55.8% of generated artifacts contained at least one identified vulnerability. 
* **Model Performance Hierarchy:** No model achieved a passing grade higher than a D. GPT-4o demonstrated the highest risk with a 62.4% vulnerability rate (Grade F), while Gemini 2.5 Flash performed relatively better at 48.4% (Grade D).
* **Failure of Industry Tooling:** A staggering 97.8% of the vulnerabilities proven by the Z3 solver were missed by a combination of six leading industry security tools.
* **The Self-Correction Paradox:** The research highlights a discrepancy in [[Machine Learning]] capabilities: while models can identify their own errors in "review mode" with 78.7% accuracy, they continue to generate them at a 55.8% rate during standard generation.
* **Ineffectiveness of Prompt Engineering:** Providing explicit security instructions to the models yielded minimal benefits, reducing the mean vulnerability rate by only 4 percentage points.

## Conclusion

The findings suggest that current [[Automated Code Generation]] technologies lack the inherent safety required for security-sensitive domains. The gap between a model's ability to recognize an error and its inability to avoid generating it remains a primary obstacle to the safe deployment of [[AI]] in mission-critical [[Software Engineering]] tasks.
