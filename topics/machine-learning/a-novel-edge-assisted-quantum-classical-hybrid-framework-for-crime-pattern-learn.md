# A Novel Edge-Assisted Quantum-Classical Hybrid Framework for Crime Pattern Learning and Classification

## Overview
As rapid urbanization accelerates, law enforcement agencies face unprecedented challenges in managing the high-dimensional and imbalanced datasets generated by increasing criminal activities. This research introduces a comparative framework designed to evaluate the efficacy of [[Quantum Machine Learning]] (QML) against traditional computational methods for [[Predictive Policing]].

## Methodology
The study utilizes 16 years of crime statistics from Bangladesh to conduct a systematic assessment of four distinct computational paradigms:
1.  **Quantum Models**: Purely quantum-based approaches.
2.  **Classical Baselines**: Traditional [[Machine Learning]] models.
3.  **Hybrid Architectures**: Two specialized quantum-classical hybrid frameworks.

The research focuses on evaluating classification performance and computational efficiency, specifically investigating the [[QAOA]] (Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm) and its ability to utilize correlation-aware circuit designs. These designs are engineered to incorporate domain-specific feature relationships directly into the quantum models.

## Key Findings
The experimental results demonstrate that quantum-inspired approaches, specifically the proposed hybrid models, can achieve an accuracy of up to 84.6%. A significant discovery is that these quantum-enhanced models require fewer trainable parameters than their classical counterparts. This reduction in complexity offers a practical advantage for deployment in memory-constrained environments.

## Implications for [[Edge Computing]]
The low computational overhead and compact parameter footprint of this framework make it an ideal candidate for [[Edge Computing]] applications. Specifically, the architecture is well-suited for deployment within wireless sensor networks used in [[Smart Cities]] surveillance systems. By enabling localized crime analytics at distributed nodes, the system minimizes communication costs and allows for real-time, distributed intelligence in urban monitoring.

## Conclusion
While this study provides a preliminary empirical assessment using structured crime data, it establishes a foundation for integrating quantum-enhanced learning into large-scale, real-world criminal analytics and distributed sensor networks. Further research is required to test these models against larger datasets and more complex, realistic quantum hardware.
