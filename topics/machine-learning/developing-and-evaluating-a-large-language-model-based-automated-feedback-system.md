# Developing and Evaluating an LLM-Based Automated Feedback System for Physics Problem Solving

This research explores the application of [[Generative AI]] within highly specialized educational contexts, specifically focusing on the challenges of providing high-quality, automated feedback for advanced [[Physics]] problem-solving. While [[Large Language Models]] (LLMs) have shown significant potential for general conceptual tasks, applying them to complex, domain-specific scientific reasoning remains a substantial technical hurdle.

## Methodology: Evidence-Centered Design

The study introduces a feedback system architecture grounded in [[Evidence-Centered Design]] (ECD). Rather than relying on unstructured prompting, the system uses the ECD framework to ensure that the feedback generated is tied to specific indicators of student competence and scientific accuracy. This approach aims to bridge the gap between general-purpose conversational ability and the rigorous requirements of advanced [[STEM]] education. The system's performance was evaluated using real-world data from the German Physics Olympiad.

## Findings and Evaluation

The evaluation revealed a dual-natured outcome regarding the utility of [[Automated Feedback]]:

1.  **Perceived Utility:** Participants generally found the feedback to be highly useful and perceived the system as having high accuracy.
2.  **Error Rate:** An in-depth analysis uncovered that the feedback contained errors in 20% of the analyzed cases.

A critical finding of the study is the "detection gap"—many of the errors produced by the LLM went unnoticed by the students. This highlights the significant risk of [[AI Hallucination]] and the potential dangers of uncritical reliance on [[Machine Learning]] outputs in academic settings.

## Future Directions

To mitigate these risks, the study suggests that future developments in [[Adaptive Learning]] must focus on creating more reliable and verifiable models. Potential research directions include the integration of more robust verification layers to ensure that feedback remains grounded in physical laws and mathematical truth, reducing the likelihood of undetected inaccuracies in automated educational tools.
