# Extracting Breast Cancer Phenotypes from Clinical Notes: Comparing LLMs with Classical Ontology Methods

## Overview
A significant portion of critical data within [[oncology|Oncology]] [[electronic-medical-records|Electronic Medical Records]] (EMRs) remains trapped in unstructured, natural language provider notes. These notes contain vital clinical insights, including tumor location, size, growth patterns, and various biomarkers. Because many oncologists prefer documenting observations in natural language rather than navigating structured EMR fields, there is a pressing need for automated [[natural-language-processing|Natural Language Processing]] (NLP) solutions to convert this text into actionable data.

## Research Objectives
The primary goal of this research is to present and evaluate an [[analyzing-multimodal-interaction-strategies-for-llm-assisted-manipulation-of-3d-|LLM]]-based framework specifically designed to extract medical knowledge and phenotypes from unstructured clinical notes. The study focuses on the domain of [[extracting-breast-cancer-phenotypes-from-clinical-notes-comparing-llms-with-clas|Breast Cancer]] to test the framework's ability to identify specific phenotypic characteristics.

To determine the efficacy of the new approach, the researchers compared the [[large-language-models-for-outpatient-referral-problem-definition-benchmarking-an|Large Language Models]] framework against traditional, knowledge-driven annotation systems, specifically the [[ncit-ontology-annotator|NCIt Ontology Annotator]] (based on the National Cancer Institute Thesaurus).

## Key Findings
The study provides several critical insights into the future of [[information-extraction|Information Extraction]] in healthcare:

* **Comparative Accuracy:** The LLM-based framework achieved extraction accuracy levels that are comparable to classical, ontology-based methods.
* **High Adaptability:** A major advantage of the LLM approach is its flexibility. Unlike rigid ontology-based systems, the LLM framework can be efficiently fine-tuned to adapt to different cancer types and various other medical diseases.
* **Scalability:** The framework demonstrates the potential to automate the processing of large-scale clinical datasets, transforming unstructured text into structured, analyzable phenotypes.

## Conclusion
This research demonstrates that [[a-comparative-analysis-of-machine-learning-models-in-shap-analysis|Machine Learning]] models can match the performance of specialized biological ontologies while offering superior versatility. The ability to easily re-train these models for different clinical contexts makes them a powerful tool for the next generation of [[clinical-informatics|Clinical Informatics]] and automated medical knowledge discovery.
