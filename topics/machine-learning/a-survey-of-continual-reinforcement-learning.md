# A Survey of Continual Reinforcement Learning

## Overview
[[Reinforcement Learning]] (RL) has become a cornerstone paradigm for addressing complex sequential decision-making problems. The integration of [[Deep Neural Networks]] has led to significant breakthroughs, allowing RL agents to master intricate tasks. However, traditional RL approaches face critical bottlenecks: they are notoriously dependent on vast amounts of training data and intensive computational power. Furthermore, standard RL models often lack the ability to [[Generalization]] across different tasks, making them difficult to deploy in the unpredictable and dynamic nature of real-world environments.

## The Rise of Continual Reinforcement Learning (CRL)
To overcome the limitations of static learning, [[Continual Reinforcement Learning]] (CRL) has emerged as a vital research direction. By incorporating elements of [[Continual Learning]] (CL), CRL aims to develop agents capable of learning from a continuous stream of information. The fundamental objective is to enable agents to adapt to new, incoming tasks while simultaneously retaining previously acquired knowledge, thereby avoiding the pitfall of catastrophic forgetting.

## Research Contributions
This survey provides a rigorous and comprehensive examination of the current CRL landscape. The authors structure their analysis into three core components:

1.  **Comprehensive Review:** The paper conducts a detailed evaluation of existing literature, organizing current research through the lens of specific metrics, task types, standardized benchmarks, and various environmental scenario settings.
2.  **New Taxonomy:** A significant contribution of this work is the proposal of a new taxonomy for CRL methodologies. The authors categorize existing methods into four distinct types, based on their specific approaches to [[Knowledge Storage]] and [[Knowledge Transfer]].
3.  **Future Directions:** The survey concludes by highlighting the unique, unresolved challenges inherent in CRL and provides actionable insights and potential trajectories for future scientific inquiry.
