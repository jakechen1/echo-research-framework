# The Rhetoric of Machine Learning

The paper "The Rhetoric of Machine Learning" offers a critical examination of [[a-comparative-analysis-of-machine-learning-models-in-shap-analysis|Machine Learning]] through the lens of rhetoric—defined as the art of persuasion. The central thesis challenges the prevailing assumption within the [[us-cities-are-axing-flock-safety-surveillance-technology|Technology]] sector that algorithmic development is a neutral or "objective" process. Instead, the author argues that the construction of "world models" from raw data is an inherently rhetorical act.

The core of the argument lies in the rejection of the idea that [[artificial-intelligence-and-the-structure-of-mathematics|Artificial Intelligence]] serves as a transparent or unbiased window into reality. The author suggests that the processes involved in training models—including feature selection, data weighting, and architectural design—carry intrinsic layers of persuasion. These choices shape how a model "argues" for certain patterns or conclusions, effectively presenting a curated version of reality as an indisputable, mathematical fact.

A significant portion of the work is dedicated to analyzing the rising business model of "manipulation as a service."
