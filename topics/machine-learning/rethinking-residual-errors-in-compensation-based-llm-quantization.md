# Rethinking Residual Errors in Compensation-based LLM Quantization

The paper "Rethinking Residual Errors in Compensation-based LLM Quantization" proposes a novel optimization strategy for the [[Quantization]] of [[Large Language Models]] (LLMs). As the scale of [[Neural Networks]] continues to grow, [[Model Compression]] techniques have become critical for deploying high-performance models on resource-constrained hardware.

### Background
Recent advancements in [[Machine Learning]] have popularized weight compensation methods to mitigate the accuracy loss caused by low-bit quantization. Landmark techniques such as [[GPTQ]] allow for efficient-parameter quantization by iteratively adjusting weights to minimize output errors. Building on this, [[GPTAQ]] introduced an asymmetric calibration process intended to align the output of quantized layers with their full-precision counterparts.

### The Problem: Sub-optimal Calibration
The authors identify a fundamental shortcoming in the calibration objectives of existing iterative weight compensation frameworks. Currently, during the intra-layer calibration process, these methods align the quantized output with the output generated by *compensated* weights, rather than the original [[Full-Precision]] model output. This divergence creates an accumulating error that deviates from the ground-truth model performance.

### Proposed Innovation: Compensation-Aware Error
To rectify this, the researchers redefine the calibration objective to ensure that the quantized model's output precisely aligns with the original output of the full-precision model at each step of the process. 

A key contribution of this work is the discovery of the **"compensation-aware error."** The authors reveal that the residual error is not solely a product of output differences from preceding layers; it also stems from the internal discrepancy between the compensated weights and the original weights within a single layer. 

### Results and Implementation
By utilizing the neuron decomposition technique inherited from [[GPTAQ]], the authors developed a method to efficiently incorporate this compensation-aware error into the weight update cycle. Extensive benchmarks across various LLM architectures demonstrate that these enhancements are compatible with both [[GPTQ]] and [[GPTAQ]], significantly boosting [[Quantization]] accuracy and performance.
