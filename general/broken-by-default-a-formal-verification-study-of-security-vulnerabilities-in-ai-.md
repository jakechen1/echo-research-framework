# Broken by Default: A Formal Verification Study of Security Vulnerables in AI-Generated Code

The research paper **"Broken by Default"** presents a rigorous, mathematical evaluation of the security reliability of [[artificial-intelligence-and-the-structure-of-mathematics|Artificial Intelligence]] coding assistants. As [[synthetic-trust-attacks-modeling-how-generative-ai-manipulates-human-decisions-i|Generative AI]] becomes increasingly integrated into the [[closed-loop-autonomous-software-development-via-jira-integrated-backlog-orchestr|Software Development]] lifecycle, the potential for introducing critical [[security|Cybersecurity]] vulnerabilities into production environments becomes a significant concern.

## Methodology

The study moves beyond traditional [[Static Analysis]], which often relies on pattern-based heuristics that can be bypassed. Instead, the researchers utilized the **COBALT analysis pipeline** to perform [[automated-conjecture-resolution-with-formal-verification|Formal Verification]] on 3,500 code artifacts generated by seven frontier [[large-language-models-for-outpatient-referral-problem-definition-benchmarking-an|Large Language Models]] (LLMs). 

By employing the [[Z3 SMT solver]], the study generated mathematical satisfiability witnesses to provide concrete proof of vulnerabilities. The dataset was comprised of 500 security-critical prompts covering five distinct Common Weakness Enumeration (CWE) categories.

## Key Findings

The study reveals a "broken by default" state for current models:

* **High Vulnerability Prevalence:** Across all tested models, an average of 55.8% of generated artifacts contained at least one identified vulnerability. 
* **Model Performance Hierarchy:** No model achieved a passing grade higher than a D. GPT-4o demonstrated the highest risk with a 62.4% vulnerability rate (Grade F), while Gemini 2.5 Flash performed relatively better at 48.4% (Grade D).
* **Failure of Industry Tooling:** A staggering 97.8% of the vulnerabilities proven by the Z3 solver were missed by a combination of six leading industry security tools.
* **The Self-Correction Paradox:** The research highlights a discrepancy in [[a-comparative-analysis-of-machine-learning-models-in-shap-analysis|Machine Learning]] capabilities: while models can identify their own errors in "review mode" with 78.7% accuracy, they continue to generate them at a 55.8% rate during standard generation.
* **Ineffectiveness of Prompt Engineering:** Providing explicit security instructions to the models yielded minimal benefits, reducing the mean vulnerability rate by only 4 percentage points.

## Conclusion

The findings suggest that current [[Automated Code Generation]] technologies lack the inherent safety required for security-sensitive domains. The gap between a model's ability to recognize an error and its inability to avoid generating it remains a primary obstacle to the safe deployment of [[a-mathematical-theory-of-evolution-for-self-designing-ais|AI]] in mission-critical [[triage-routing-software-engineering-tasks-to-cost-effective-llm-tiers-via-code-q|Software Engineering]] tasks.
