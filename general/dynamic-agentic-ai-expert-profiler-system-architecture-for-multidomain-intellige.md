# Dynamic Agentic AI Expert Profiler System Architecture

## Overview
In the evolving landscape of [[artificial-intelligence-and-the-structure-of-mathematics|Artificial Intelligence]], the ability for systems to engage in meaningful [[human-computer-interactions-predict-mental-health|Human-Computer Interaction]] (HCI) depends heavily on context awareness. The "Dynamic Agentic AI Expert Profiler" is a framework designed to bridge the gap between machine responses and human expertise levels. By identifying the skill level of a user through natural language, the system can adapt its communication style to be more effective across diverse populations.

## System Architecture
The proposed system utilizes a modular, layered architecture built upon the [[LLaMB v3.1 (8B)]] large language model. The architecture is designed to process natural language inputs through a structured pipeline consisting of:
* **Text Preprocessing**: Cleaning and preparing raw input.
* **Scoring**: Analyzing linguistic markers of expertise.
* **Aggregation**: Synthesizing data across multiple inputs.
* **Classification**: Final assignment of a user profile.

The system classifies users into four distinct proficiency tiers:
1. [[Novice]]
2. [[Basic]]
3. [[spatiotemporal-aware-bit-flip-injection-on-dnn-based-advanced-driver-assistance-|Advanced]]
4. [[dynamic-agentic-ai-expert-profiler-system-architecture-for-multidomain-intellige|Expert]]

## Methodology and Evaluation
The researchers evaluated the profiler through a dual-phase approach:
* **Static Phase**: Analyzed pre-recorded transcripts from 82 participants to establish baseline accuracy.
* **Dynamic Phase**: Deployed an [[dynamic-agentic-ai-expert-profiler-system-architecture-for-multidomain-intellige|Agentic AI]] interviewer to conduct 402 live interviews. Unlike the static phase, the dynamic phase assessed expertise level incrementally after each individual response, allowing for real-time profile updates.

## Results and Limitations
The profiler demonstrated high reliability, with accuracy rates ranging from 83% to 97% when compared against participant self-assessments. These results suggest that [[Natural Language Processing]] can effectively map linguistic complexity to domain knowledge.

However, the study identified several challenges in maintaining perfect accuracy:
* **Self-rating Bias**: Discrepancies between how users perceive themselves and their actual demonstrated skill.
* **Ambiguity**: Unclear or low-information responses from participants.
* **Nuance Misinterpretation**: Occasional failures in the [[from-large-language-model-predicates-to-logic-tensor-networks-neurosymbolic-offe|Large Language Model]] to detect subtle indicators of high-level professional expertise.
