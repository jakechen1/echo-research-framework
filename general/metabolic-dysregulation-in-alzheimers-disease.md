## Overview

Metabolic dysregulation in Alzheimer's Disease (AD) refers to the profound alteration in the biochemical fluxes and nutrient-sensing pathways that sustain neuronal viability, synaptic plasticity, and cellular homeostasis. While historically AD has been characterized primarily by the accumulation of amyloid-beta ($\text{A}\beta$) plaques and neurofibrillary tangles of hyperphosphorylated tau, a growing consensus in modern neuroscience posits that metabolic failure is not merely a secondary consequence of proteinopathy, but a primary driver of the disease pathology. This metabolic decline often manifests as "glucose hypometabolism," a state where the brain's ability to utilize glucose for ATP production is significantly compromised, long before the onset of overt clinical symptoms.

The dysregulation encompasses a wide spectrum of metabolic processes, including impaired glycolysis, mitochondrial dysfunction, altered amino acid biosynthesis, and disrupted lipid signaling. This systemic breakdown of bioenergetic stability leads to a state of "nutritional stress" within the neural microenvironment, fundamentally altering the way neurons and glia interact to maintain cognitive function.

## The Bioenergetic Crisis: Mitochondrial Dysfunction and Oxidative Stress

At the core of AD-related metabolic failure is the breakdown of mitochondrial efficiency. As neurons are highly energy-dependent cells, any disruption in the oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) pathway results in immediate consequences for synaptic maintenance. Recent research suggests that mitochondrial dysfunction is a hallmark of the early stages of AD, serving as a precursor to the more widespread neurode None-degeneration.

Research conducted by D'Alessandro et al. (2025) has highlighted that mitochondrial dysfunction in AD is characterized by a significant decrease in the efficiency of the electron transport chain (ETC) and an increase in the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS). This bioenergetic failure leads to a depletion of cellular ATP, which impairs ATP-dependent processes such as the maintenance of ion gradients (e.g., via the $\text{Na}^+/\text{K}^+$-ATPase pump) and the recycling of neurotransmitters. Furthermore, the accumulation of mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) mutations and the impairment of mitophagy (the selective degradation of damaged mitochondria) exacerbate the presence of dysfunctional organelles, creating a cycle of oxidative stress and cellular decay.

## Altered Amino Acid Flux: The Serine Pathway and PHGDH

A critical dimension of metabolic dysregulation in AD is the alteration of amino acid biosynthetic pathways, particularly those involving the serine biosynthetic pathway. Serine is a vital amino acid that serves as a precursor for various essential molecules, including glycine, cysteine, and neurotransmitters like D-serine, which acts as a co-agonist at NMDA receptors.

A key metabolic enzyme in this pathway, 3-phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase (PHGDH), has emerged as a significant player in the AD landscape. The [[Role of PHGDH in Alzheimer's Disease Pathogenesis]] is increasingly scrutinized, as fluctuations in PHGDH activity can significantly alter the metabolic flux of glycolytic intermediates into the serine biosynthetic pathway. When this pathway is compromised, the resulting depletion of serine and its derivatives can lead to significant neurobiological deficits.

Specifically, the [[Impact of Serine Deficiency on Synaptic Plasticity]] is a critical area of investigation. A shortage of serine-derived neurotransmitters and metabolic precursors disrupts the long-term potentiation (LTP) necessary for memory formation. This deficiency also affects the production of glutathione, a primary antioxidant, thereby linking amino acid dysregulation back to the oxidative stress caused by mitochondrial failure.

## Epigenetic-Metabolic Interplay: Acetyl-CoA and ACSS2

Metabolism and epigenetics are inextricably linked through the availability of metabolic substrates that serve as substrates for chromatin-modifying enzymes. One of the most significant links in the AD brain is the availability of Acetyl-CoA, which is required for histone acetylation—a process essential for regulating the expression of genes related to memory and synaptic plasticity.

Recent studies have identified the role of Acetyl-CoA synthetase 2 (ACSS2) in this context. ACSS2 is a key enzyme that converts acetate into Acetyl-CoA in the nucleus. Research by Lin Y et al. (2023) has demonstrated that ACSS2-dependent histone acetylation is a critical mechanism for maintaining cognitive function. In mouse models of Alzheimer's disease, the enhancement of ACSS2 activity—and the resulting increase in histone acetylation—has been shown to improve cognitive outcomes. This suggests that the "metabolic-epigenetic" axis is a vulnerable target in AD, where a lack of acetate-derived Acetyl-CoA leads to a repressive epigenetic state that shuts down essential neuroplasticity genes.

## Spatial Heterogeneity in Metabolic Dysfunction

One of the most significant challenges in studying metabolic dysregulation is the highly heterogeneous nature of the AD brain. The metabolic state of a neuron located in a healthy region of the hippocampus may differ drastically from a neuron adjacent to an $\text{A}\beta$ plaque.

The advent of spatial transcriptomics and in situ sequencing has revolutionized our ability to map these metabolic changes with high precision. As demonstrated by Chen WT et al. (2020), spatial transcriptomics allows researchers to observe how metabolic gene expression patterns change in direct relation to the presence of pathology. These technologies have revealed that the metabolic "fingerprint" of AD is spatially organized; for instance, there is a localized downregulation of glycolytic and oxidative phosphorylation genes in the immediate vicinity of amyloid plaques, creating a "metabolic desert" surrounding the protein aggregates. This spatial understanding is vital for developing targeted therapies that can address localized metabolic crises without disrupting systemic metabolism.

## Current Challenges and Future Directions

Despite significant progress, several challenges remain in the field of neuro-metabolism:

1.  **Causality vs. Consequence:** It remains difficult to definitively determine whether metabolic dysregulation is the primary driver of AD or a secondary effect of $\text{A}\beta$ and Tau-induced toxicity.
2.  **Cell-Type Specificity:** Much of our understanding is derived from bulk tissue analysis or, more recently, single-cell sequencing. However, the metabolic interplay between neurons, astrocytes, and microglia—specifically the "metabolic coupling" where astrocytes provide lactate to neurons—is incredibly complex and requires more detailed investigation.
3.  **Therapeutic Translation:** While targeting metabolic enzymes like ACSS2 or restoring serine levels shows promise in preclinical models, translating these findings into human clinical trials remains a monumental task due to the blood-brain barrier and the systemic side effects of altering global metabolism.

The future of the field lies in the integration of multi-omic data (proteomics, metabolomics, and transcriptomics) with spatial imaging. The goal is to develop "metabolic-state" biomarkers that can detect the earliest shifts in glycolytic or amino acid flux, enabling much earlier intervention than is currently possible with traditional amyloid-based imaging.
